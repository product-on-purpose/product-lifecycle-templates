## Honest framing (the through-line for the companion)

A risk register is the **canonical governance instrument for tracking risk**, and it is also the artifact
most vulnerable to becoming **"risk theater"**: a static list created at kickoff, filed, and never revisited
until the next audit, with `high`/`low` scores that a serious body of work argues are mathematically
dubious. This bundle teaches the register that **survives that critique**: living (reviewed on a cadence and
on triggers), objective-anchored, owner-assigned, scored against **defined** scales, and carrying a
**residual** score (after controls), not just an inherent one. The contested core is stated honestly, not
hidden: likelihood-times-impact heat maps are under sustained, credentialed attack, and a best-in-class
template has to teach the register that works anyway.

**The load-bearing honest-retrieval fact (do not get this wrong):** ISO 31000 does **not** name or mandate
a "risk register." The one-line definition everyone quotes ("a record of information about identified
risks") is from **ISO Guide 73:2009**, the vocabulary companion, not from ISO 31000. Practitioner and vendor
pages routinely misattribute it. The companion states this correctly and makes it a teaching point.

**Sharpest teaching points:**
1. A **risk** is a potential future event; an **issue** is one that has already materialized. The register
   tracks risks; a materialized risk moves to an **issue log**.
2. The risk register is the **"R" of a RAID log**. Standalone register vs RAID log is a choice by **audience
   and cadence**: a weekly team working document vs a quarterly steering-committee governance artifact.
3. A well-formed risk is a **cause -> event -> consequence** statement, not a vague theme label.
4. **Inherent vs residual** risk (before controls vs after) is "the most consequential distinction"; a
   register with only one score cannot show whether its controls work.
5. A register is a **living tool, not a filing cabinet**; out-of-date scoring is the most common failure.
6. **Risk owner vs risk actionee**: the owner owns the objective and the risk; the actionee executes an
   assigned response action.
7. Response strategies: **avoid, mitigate/reduce, transfer, accept** (PMBOK 6+ adds **escalate**); for
   opportunities, the mirror five, **escalate, exploit, share, enhance, accept** (source [17] lists escalate
   on both sides; do not drop it from the opportunity list).

## Claims flagged contested or time-bound

- **ISO 31000 does not name a "risk register"** [1][3]. The quoted one-line definition is ISO Guide 73:2009
  [2], the vocabulary companion. Citing "ISO 31000 requires a register" is the common error; the companion
  states this correctly.
- **Four vs five response strategies** [17]. The classic model is four (avoid, mitigate, transfer, accept);
  PMBOK 6 (2017)+ adds "escalate." Both are in live use.
- **Likelihood x impact heat maps: "worse than useless" vs "one of the best practical tools"** [23][27]. A
  genuine, unresolved debate. Cox [23] and Hubbard [24][33] attack the math; Talbot [27] defends the tool in
  practice. The companion presents both camps and takes the constructive position (anchor the scales, use
  the score to drive discussion, do not treat it as precise math).
- **Does agile eliminate the register?** [28][29]. Cohn [28]: largely unnecessary for small/medium agile;
  ISACA [29]: still record risks in a register. Scope-dependent (team vs program) and regulation-dependent.
- **Risk score color-zone thresholds are not standardized** [15][16]. Bands are a local choice anchored to
  the organization's risk appetite; no banding is canonical.
- **Scoring color zones in [16] are ISO 45001 (safety) bands** and are not the project/enterprise norm.
- **PMBOK 7 repositioned the register** from a mandatory process output to a "commonly used" artifact; whether
  that is de-emphasis or repositioning is itself debated. Stated as repositioning, sourced to the edition
  shift, not overclaimed.
