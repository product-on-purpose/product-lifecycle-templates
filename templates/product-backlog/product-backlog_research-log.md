Quotable verbatim (read on page): "an emergent, ordered list of what is needed to improve the product"; "It is the single source of work undertaken by the Scrum Team."; "Product Backlog refinement is the act of breaking down and further defining Product Backlog items into smaller more precise items. ### Structure and management

8. **[Tier 2] Cohn, Mike. 11. **[Tier 2] Pichler, Roman. 12. **[Tier 2] Pichler, Roman. 13. **[Tier 2] Pichler, Roman. ## Claims flagged contested or time-bound

- **"Ordered" vs "prioritized"** (sources 1, 3, 7, 10): the 2020 Scrum Guide deliberately uses "ordered"; most practitioner writing (Pichler, DEEP) still says "prioritized". Whether this is a meaningful distinction or a branding change is genuinely contested; no primary source adjudicates it.
- **Who coined DEEP** (source 8): co-credited to Cohn and Pichler; sole authorship not settled.
- **Refinement cadence** (sources 9, 10): Cohn says ~10 percent of sprint effort; Pichler says at least once per sprint; the Scrum Guide prescribes none. Compatible in spirit, different in specificity.
- **Is the backlog Scrum-specific or general agile** (sources 14, 17, 18): Scrum formalized the named artifact; XP predates it with story cards; Kanban rejects the term ("options"); SAFe scales it. Treated as a general agile concept that Scrum named.
- **Outcome over output / is the backlog being displaced** (sources 21-24, 27): a live debate with named camps (Cagan/SVPG displacement via opportunity backlogs; Torres evolution via discovery; Pichler balance; Jeffries radical critique). The companion presents the camps and recommends anchoring to a Product Goal rather than picking a winner.
- **No-estimates vs story points** (source 25): Jeffries's regret targets misuse; the defenders' camp was not fetched, so the companion presents Jeffries's position as one camp, not the settled view.
- **Definition of Ready** (sources 29, 30): the 2020 Scrum Guide defines no DoR; practitioners split between utility (Pichler) and caution (Cohn). Presented as contested.
- **Backlog-bankruptcy figures** (source 28): the 2-4 year / 50-75 percent figures are practitioner-survey-based, not peer-reviewed.

## Notes for the companion

- Frame it honestly: the Product Backlog is Scrum-canonical (the 2020 Guide's one of three artifacts, with the Product Goal as its commitment) but is a general agile concept with real variants (Kanban "options", SAFe ART/Portfolio backlogs, XP story cards), and it is under sustained outcome-over-output pressure. The bundle teaches the goal-anchored, ordered, emergent backlog with the debates surfaced, not flattened.
- The load-bearing template sections: the Product Goal (commitment), the ordered Backlog Items list (DEEP, typed items), the ordering rationale (ordered not prioritized), refinement and readiness (iceberg, DoR), and for the full variant a prioritization framework, dependencies, and backlog-health metrics.
- The sharpest teaching points: (1) ordered, not prioritized (and why the distinction matters); (2) a backlog is output, so anchor it to a Product Goal or it becomes a feature factory; (3) backlog vs roadmap (tactical output list vs strategic outcome plan). These belong in the companion's debates and relationships sections and the guide's anti-patterns.
