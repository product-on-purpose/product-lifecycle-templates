## Sources

### Canon and composition

1. **[Tier 1] Schwaber, Ken and Sutherland, Jeff. "The 2020 Scrum Guide."** https://scrumguides.org/scrum-guide.html - **fetched-and-verified** - Supports the canonical composition (Sprint Goal (why) + selected Product Backlog items (what) + an actionable plan (how)), ownership by the Developers, the real-time living nature, the Daily Scrum connection, the Sprint Goal as the commitment, forecast language, mid-Sprint scope renegotiation, and burndown deprescribed. Quotable (read on page): "The Sprint Backlog is composed of the Sprint Goal (why), the set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint (what), as well as an actionable plan for delivering the Increment (how)."; "The Sprint Backlog is a plan by and for the Developers."; "a highly visible, real-time picture of the work that the Developers plan to accomplish during the Sprint in order to achieve the Sprint Goal"; "the Sprint Backlog is updated throughout the Sprint as more is learned. It should have enough detail that they can inspect their progress in the Daily Scrum."; "the single objective for the Sprint"; "provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it"; "creates coherence and focus, encouraging the Scrum Team to work together rather than on separate initiatives"; "Scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the Product Owner as more is learned."; "The more the Developers know about their past performance, their upcoming capacity, and their Definition of Done, the more confident they will be in their Sprint forecasts."; "Various practices exist to forecast progress, like burn-downs, burn-ups, or cumulative flows. While proven useful, these do not replace the importance of empiricism."

## Claims flagged contested or time-bound

- **"Forecast" versus "commitment"** (sources 1, 3, 5, 11, 12): the 2020 Scrum Guide uses "forecast" for the selected work (a change made in 2011); the *commitment* it names is the Sprint Goal. Cohn keeps "commitment" but qualifies it ("not a guarantee", team-to-team); Atlassian says teams "commit to shipping", which conflicts with the Guide. The template uses "forecast" as canonical and notes "commitment" as a qualified practitioner convention.
- **"Yesterday's weather"** (source 6): the concept (past velocity predicts the next sprint) is fetched-and-verified; the *phrase* is not verbatim on any page read, so it is explained, not quoted or attributed to a URL.
- **Velocity as input, output, or target** (sources 5, 6, 11): genuinely different approaches. Capacity-driven (Cohn's stated preference) uses velocity only as a post-selection check; the vendor guide uses it as the primary input. "Velocity is a measure, not a target" is a widely held practitioner caution.
- **Sprint Goal consent versus acceptance** (sources 1, 10): Pichler seeks consent (nobody objects) and says never force a goal; the Guide frames the Developers as committing to the goal by Sprint start.
- **Burndown's role** (sources 1, 7): the 2020 Guide deprescribed burndown as required; vendor tools (Jira) still default to it. The template presents both realities.
- **Scope change mid-sprint** (source 1): the 2020 Guide explicitly allows renegotiating scope with the Product Owner as more is learned, without affecting the Sprint Goal; some older-edition practitioners still treat sprint scope as locked.
- The **2017 PDF** (source 2) was paraphrased by the fetch layer, so nothing is quoted from it; it supports only the pre-2020 structural contrast, corroborated by the revisions page (source 3).

## Notes for the companion

- Frame it honestly: the Sprint Backlog is the **Developers' living plan** for one Sprint, composed of the Sprint Goal (why) + selected items (what) + the delivery plan (how), a **forecast, not a contract**. Its commitment is the Sprint Goal, which fixes the *objective* while leaving the exact work flexible.
- The load-bearing sections: the Sprint Goal, the selected items (drawn from the product backlog), and the delivery plan; the full variant adds capacity and forecast, progress and tracking, and risks and carry-over.
- The sharpest teaching points: (1) it is **by and for the Developers**, not the Product Owner's list; (2) it is a **forecast, and the commitment is the Sprint Goal**, not the item list; (3) it is **drawn from the product backlog** (a Sprint-sized subset), and its increment must meet the Definition of Done. Chains with the product-backlog bundle (its upstream) on the Saved Views scenario.
