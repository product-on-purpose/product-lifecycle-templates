# Master Catalog of Product Management and SDLC Document and Artifact Types

## TL;DR
- This catalog consolidates **205 distinct, deduplicated artifact TYPES** (with aliases folded into single entries) spanning the entire arc from idea to end-of-life, grouped into 19 categories; the **core 28-type "must-have" tier** (vision, strategy, roadmap, business case, PRD, persona, user story, backlogs, acceptance criteria/DoD, OKRs, wireframe, prototype, design doc, ADR, test plan/case, bug report, release notes, runbook, postmortem, status report, retro notes, dashboard, RAID/risk register) earns its place in roughly 80% of organizations.
- The long tail is dominated by **methodology-specific artifacts** (Amazon PR/FAQ and six-page narrative memo, Shape Up pitch/breadboard/hill chart, SAFe PI objectives/program board/ROAM board, Scrum increment/Definition of Done) and **regulated-industry artifacts** (FDA design control records per 21 CFR 820.30, IQ/OQ/PQ process validation, ISO 14971 Risk Management File, SOC 2 system description/control matrix, GDPR Records of Processing/DPIA), most of which are rare outside their domain and flagged accordingly.
- A Completeness Pass was performed across all seven Coverage Matrix axes; adding further items would only repeat existing entries or aliases, so the stopping condition is met. The catalog is ready to drive a template repository with S/M/L size variants per type.

## Key Findings
- **"Document type" volume is genuinely in the hundreds, not dozens.** Once aliases are consolidated and methodology variants, role-specific outputs, ceremony inputs/outputs, and regulated artifacts are included, the deduplicated count reaches 205. The obvious "PM 20" (PRD, roadmap, backlog, etc.) is a small fraction.
- **Aliases are the biggest source of double-counting.** PRD = product spec = product brief = one-pager (size variants of one type); runbook vs. playbook; postmortem vs. RCA vs. after-action review; RFC vs. ADR vs. design doc (related but distinct). These are resolved in the Alias Index.
- **Authoritative sources differ on whether some artifacts are "real" or deprecated.** Marty Cagan (SVPG) argues the PRD is being overused as a substitute for discovery and that "the majority of the product spec should be the high-fidelity prototype"; the Scrum Guide 2020 recognizes only three formal artifacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment) with three commitments (Product Goal, Sprint Goal, Definition of Done), treating burndown charts and roadmaps as non-canonical extensions.
- **Regulated artifacts are mandatory, auditable, and rarely optional.** Under FDA 21 CFR 820.30, the regulation states verbatim that "Each manufacturer shall establish and maintain a DHF [Design History File] for each type of device." Effective **February 2, 2026**, FDA's revised Part 820 is retitled the **Quality Management System Regulation (QMSR)**, incorporating ISO 13485:2016 by reference; under the harmonized § 820.3 the traditional Device Master Record (DMR), Device History Record (DHR), and Design History File (DHF) terms align to the unified **Medical Device File (MDF)** per ISO 13485, and QSIT was retired and replaced by Compliance Program 7382.850.
- **Test documentation standards have shifted.** IEEE 829-2008 (Test Documentation) has been superseded by the **ISO/IEC/IEEE 29119-3** series (latest 29119-3:2021); IEEE 829-era artifacts (Master Test Plan, Test Design Spec, Test Case Spec, Test Incident Report) are now legacy terminology though still widely used in practice.

## Recommendations
Staged, concrete next steps for building the template repository:

1. **Build the must-have 28 first (Tier 1).** These cover ~80% of organizations and unblock the broadest set of teams. Ship each with S/M/L variants where the schema flags variant potential (e.g., PRD as one-pager/standard/full; runbook as quick-reference/standard/comprehensive). *Threshold to proceed to Tier 2:* Tier 1 templates adopted by at least one team each and surviving one real usage cycle.
2. **Add Tier 2 by domain pull, not all at once.** Layer in methodology packs (Scrum, SAFe, Shape Up, Amazon working-backwards) and discipline packs (UX, data/analytics, security, release/ops) based on which teams actually request them. *Threshold:* only build a methodology pack when a team is actively practicing it; otherwise the templates become shelfware.
3. **Treat regulated artifacts (Tier 3) as a separate, access-controlled module.** FDA design-control records, ISO 14971 RMF, SOC 2, and GDPR artifacts are auditable and must be version-controlled with change history and approval workflows. *Critical benchmark:* if you operate in health/finance/gov/safety-critical domains, these are mandatory, not optional, and should be templated with built-in traceability fields. Verify the current operative regulation text on ecfr.gov before finalizing FDA templates, since the QMSR amendment took effect February 2, 2026 and changes terminology (DHF/DMR/DHR aligning to the ISO 13485 Medical Device File).
4. **Encode the alias index as machine-readable metadata so naming variance never fragments the repo.** Every template's frontmatter should list its aliases; a search for "product spec" must resolve to the PRD template.
5. **Default to the smallest useful variant.** Cagan's caution that PRDs can become a substitute for discovery applies broadly: bias templates toward lightweight S variants with explicit "scale up only if" guidance, and make the high-fidelity prototype (not prose) the center of gravity for product specs.
6. **Re-validate the catalog annually.** Standards shift (IEEE 829 to ISO/IEC/IEEE 29119; Part 820 to QMSR). Schedule a yearly review to retire legacy templates and fold in new artifact types (e.g., AI/ML model cards, which are emerging but not yet universal).

## Repo-Architecture Recommendation (Appendix)
- **Folder taxonomy:** top-level folders mirror the 19 Matrix-E categories (`/01-strategy-vision`, `/02-market-competitive`, ... `/19-legal-contractual`). Within each, one folder per canonical artifact, containing `S/`, `M/`, `L/` variant files plus a `meta.yaml`.
- **Naming convention:** `category-canonicalname-size.ext` (e.g., `requirements-prd-M.md`); regulated artifacts prefix with domain (`reg-fda-dhf-L.md`). Dated decision files follow `YYYYMMDD-title.md` (the MADR/ADR convention).
- **Machine-readable manifest (frontmatter):** each template carries YAML frontmatter with `canonical_name`, `aliases[]`, `category`, `lifecycle_phase`, `primary_owner`, `methodology`, `formality`, `rarity`, `size_variant`, `relationships[]`, `source_refs[]`, `last_reviewed`.
- **Alias index format:** a single `alias-index.json` mapping every alias string to its canonical artifact path, enabling search and preventing duplicate creation. Example: `{"product spec": "requirements/prd", "product brief": "requirements/prd", "RCA": "operations-incident/postmortem"}`.
- **Cross-references:** use the `relationships[]` field to render a dependency graph (e.g., Vision -> Strategy -> Roadmap -> PRD -> Design Doc -> ADR -> Test Plan), so authors can navigate the natural document lineage.

## Caveats
- **Source quality varies by category.** Methodology and standards artifacts (Scrum Guide, SAFe framework, Basecamp Shape Up, Amazon Working Backwards book, Google SRE Workbook, IIBA BABOK, PMBOK 7, FDA eCFR, ISO) are primary/authoritative. Some PM-template and aliasing details draw on practitioner sources (Lenny's Newsletter, SVPG/Cagan, Atlassian, Aha!, Reforge) and vendor blogs (Secureframe, Vanta, Drata for SOC 2), which are reliable but commercially motivated; treat their structural advice as convention, not standard.
- **The QMSR transition detail carries a recency flag.** The February 2, 2026 effective date for the renamed Quality Management System Regulation and the DHF/DMR/DHR-to-Medical Device File alignment was confirmed against FDA primary sourcing, but the precise post-amendment regulatory text and terminology mapping should be verified directly on ecfr.gov before producing audit-grade templates, as mirrors may still show legacy 820.30 language.
- **Boundaries between some types are genuinely contested.** PRD vs. user stories vs. high-fidelity prototype (Cagan argues the prototype should be the spec); RFC vs. ADR vs. design doc; runbook vs. playbook vs. SOP; postmortem vs. RCA vs. after-action review. These are consolidated here with the dominant convention noted, but organizations legitimately draw the lines differently.
- **Scrum's formal artifact count is narrow by design.** The Scrum Guide 2020 recognizes only three artifacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment) plus three commitments; burndown charts, sprint goals-as-artifacts, and roadmaps are widely used extensions, not canon. They are catalogued here because they are real and reusable, with the non-canonical status noted.
- **Rarity ratings are directional, not statistical.** "Common / occasional / rare-or-legacy" reflects cross-source consensus and the lead author's judgment, not a measured survey; rarity also shifts by industry (e.g., IQ/OQ/PQ is rare overall but mandatory in medical-device manufacturing).
- **No filled instances are included.** Per scope, this catalogs reusable TYPES only; specific completed documents, pure code, generic HR/finance docs, and unrelated marketing collateral are excluded by design.
